Background

Joy Buolamwini was born on July 27, 1989 in Edmonton, Canada. She is of Ghanaian descent. She moved to the United States when she was four years old and grew up in Mississippi and Georgia. She attended the Georgia Institute of Technology, where she earned her bachelor's degree in computer science with a minor in mathematics. She also attended Oxford University as a Rhodes Scholar, where she earned her master's degree in African studies. She is currently pursuing her PhD in computer science at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology.

Joy Buolamwini is the founder and leader of the Algorithmic Justice League, a research and advocacy organization that aims to expose and challenge the harms of artificial intelligence, especially facial recognition systems. She is also a poet of code, a term she coined to describe her artistic and creative approach to coding. She is also a producer and featured subject of the documentary Coded Bias, which premiered at the Sundance Film Festival in 2020.

Contributions

Joy Buolamwini is best known for her work on exposing and challenging the racial and gender bias in facial recognition systems. She conducted a series of studies that revealed how these systems fail to accurately detect and classify faces of people with darker skin tones and women, especially women of color. She also showed how these systems can have serious consequences for human rights, privacy, security, and democracy.

Joy Buolamwini's work has influenced public awareness, media, education, and policy. She has published her findings in academic journals and conferences, such as ACM FAT* and IEEE CVPR. She has also written op-eds and articles for popular outlets such as The New York Times, Time, Wired, etc. She has also given TED talks and lectures at various events and institutions around the world. She has also testified before the U.S. Congress and the European Parliament on issues related to facial recognition regulation and oversight.

Challenges

Joy Buolamwini has faced many challenges and obstacles as a woman of color in academia and technology. She has experienced racism, sexism, harassment, and discrimination from peers, colleagues, reviewers, funders, etc. She has also faced resistance and backlash from powerful corporations and institutions that profit from or rely on facial recognition systems. She has also had to cope with stress, burnout, and trauma from her work and activism.
